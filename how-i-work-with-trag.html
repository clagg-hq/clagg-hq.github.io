How to work with Chris Trag

Chris moves quickly, has strong taste, and usually prefers a direct path over a perfect process. The best way to work with him is to bring clarity early, keep the signal high, and avoid adding ceremony where a concrete next step would do.

Pace

He works fast once the shape is clear.

Chris is generally not blocked by volume. He is blocked by vagueness, drag, and work that arrives without a clear point of view. If you bring him something messy but real, he can work with that. If you bring him something polished but hollow, it will usually slow the conversation down.

A good draft, a sharp recommendation, or a clean framing of the decision tends to move things forward quickly. Long preambles do not.

Communication

Direct is better than performative.

The tone that works best is clear, concrete, and low-fluff. Chris responds well to people who know what they are trying to say and say it plainly. He does not need a lot of positioning language around the point. He would usually rather hear the real tradeoff than a padded version designed to sound polished.

If you disagree, say so. If something is weak, say what is weak about it. If a recommendation is obvious, lead with it.

Decision-making

Bring a recommendation, not just a menu.

Chris is very comfortable with strategy, product, writing, developer ecosystems, and go-to-market tradeoffs. He can evaluate options quickly. What helps most is when someone does the first pass of thinking before handing the problem over. That means framing the decision, naming the tradeoffs, and stating what you think should happen.

He is usually not looking for an exhaustive inventory of possibilities. He is looking for a sensible path, why it is the right one, and what happens next.

Craft

Taste matters.

Chris has strong editorial and design instincts. He notices when language is padded, when structure is mushy, and when something feels overworked. He values precision, restraint, and work that feels considered rather than decorated.

In practice, that means fewer slogans, fewer abstractions, fewer ornamental systems, and more evidence that the work has been thought through.

Collaboration

Autonomy is useful. Invisible assumptions are not.

Chris does not need constant check-ins for routine moves. He appreciates people who can run with something. At the same time, he cares about alignment. If the stakes are real, or if the work changes the frame of the project, it is better to surface that early than to reveal it after a lot of effort has gone into the wrong direction.

A strong collaborator here is someone who can operate independently, keep the state of the work legible, and tighten the feedback loop when it matters.

Friction

What tends to go badly.

Things usually bog down when work arrives as a wall of generic language, when the real decision is hidden, when next steps are left implied, or when people optimize for presentation over substance. Over-explaining can be as unhelpful as under-thinking.

If you want to be useful, remove ambiguity, show your reasoning, and leave the work in a state where someone can pick it up and move.

Short version

The practical version.

Bring the real problem. Say what you think. Write clearly. Prefer specifics to framing language. Keep the work moving. If there is a better path, point to it plainly.
